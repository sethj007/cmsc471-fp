FluLines

Every year, the influenza H3N2 virus mutates, travels across continents, and returns in an altered form. This is not random — it follows patterns shaped by geography, immunity, and evolution. Scroll to trace H3N2's global journey from 2010 to today, then explore the evolutionary tree that shows how each strain is related.

2010 — H3N2 Re-emerges

After the 2009 H1N1 "swine flu" pandemic dominated global headlines, H3N2 quietly reasserted itself as the dominant seasonal influenza strain. Surveillance networks across North America, Europe, and Asia began tracking its spread with renewed urgency. The map shows early but widespread H3N2 activity — a virus already present on every inhabited continent.

2014 — The Vaccine Mismatch

2014 marked one of the most significant H3N2 events in recent memory: a major antigenic drift caused the season's flu vaccine to be poorly matched to circulating strains. The virus had mutated faster than vaccine production cycles could keep up with. Case counts surged across North America and Asia, and the mismatch prompted a global reassessment of how vaccines are selected each year.

2017 — One of the Worst Seasons in Decades

Another vaccine mismatch year. H3N2 strains circulating in 2017 diverged significantly from the vaccine reference strain, resulting in one of the lowest vaccine effectiveness rates ever recorded. Hospitalizations spiked in the United States, Australia, and Europe. This season became a case study in the difficulty of predicting influenza evolution.

2020 — COVID Nearly Eliminated the Flu

Something unprecedented happened in 2020: H3N2 almost disappeared. Masks, lockdowns, school closures, and reduced international travel — measures taken to slow COVID-19 — also decimated influenza transmission globally. Reported H3N2 cases dropped to historic lows across most of the world. The map tells a striking story: a virus that had circulated every year for decades was suddenly almost absent.

2024 — The Rebound

As pandemic-era measures lifted, H3N2 rebounded — but not identically to before. Years of reduced circulation meant population immunity had waned, and the virus had continued evolving in the strains that did circulate. Today, surveillance networks track thousands of sequences per year to stay ahead of the next potential mismatch. The fight against H3N2 is ongoing.
