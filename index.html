The Flu Never Stops Evolving

Every year, the H3N2 influenza virus mutates, migrates across continents, and returns in an altered form. This visualization traces its global journey — from evolutionary family tree to real-world case counts — across nearly two decades.

ABOUT THIS PROJECT

What is FluLines?

FluLines is an interactive data visualization exploring the phylogeography of H3N2 influenza — the dominant seasonal flu subtype responsible for most severe flu seasons. Phylogeography is the study of how evolutionary history and geographic spread are intertwined: where a virus mutates, and how those mutations travel across the world.

This project was built to make that invisible process visible. Using genomic sequence data and global surveillance records, FluLines lets you watch H3N2 evolve in real time — branch by branch, year by year, country by country.

THE DATA

Two Datasets, One Story

NEXTSTRAIN · GISAID

Phylogenetic Tree

1,697 H3N2 hemagglutinin (HA) sequences sampled globally from 2008 to 2026. Each sequence is a virus collected from a patient, processed into a timetree showing evolutionary relationships and divergence dates.

1,697 sequences · 18 years
WHO FLUNET

Global Surveillance

Weekly country-level influenza surveillance data from the WHO's FluNet system, covering laboratory-confirmed H3N2 detections across 100+ countries from 1997 to present.

100+ countries · weekly reports
KEY FINDINGS

What the Data Reveals

2014

The Vaccine Mismatch

A rapid antigenic drift in the 3C.2a clade caused the 2014–15 flu vaccine to be poorly matched to circulating strains — one of the worst mismatches in decades. Case counts surged across North America and Asia.

2017

History Repeats

Another mismatch year. The 3C.2a1 sub-clade diverged significantly from the vaccine reference strain, producing one of the lowest vaccine effectiveness rates ever recorded. Hospitalizations spiked globally.

2020

COVID Killed the Flu

Pandemic-era measures — masks, lockdowns, reduced travel — nearly eliminated H3N2 globally. Reported cases dropped to historic lows. The phylogenetic tree shows an evolutionary bottleneck: very few strains survived to 2021.

2022+

The Rebound & New Clades

As immunity waned and travel resumed, H3N2 rebounded rapidly under new clade designations (2a.1b.2a, 2a.1b.2b). The virus that returned post-pandemic was evolutionarily distinct from the one that disappeared in 2020.

THE STORY

2010 — H3N2 Re-emerges

After the 2009 H1N1 pandemic, H3N2 quietly reasserted itself as the dominant seasonal strain. Surveillance networks began tracking its spread with renewed urgency.

2014 — The Vaccine Mismatch

A major antigenic drift caused the flu vaccine to be poorly matched to circulating strains. Case counts surged across North America and Asia.

2017 — One of the Worst Seasons in Decades

Another mismatch year. One of the lowest vaccine effectiveness rates ever recorded. Hospitalizations spiked in the US, Australia, and Europe.

2020 — COVID Nearly Eliminated the Flu

H3N2 almost disappeared. Pandemic measures decimated influenza transmission globally. Cases dropped to historic lows across most of the world.

2024 — The Rebound

H3N2 rebounded as pandemic measures lifted. Surveillance networks now track thousands of sequences per year to stay ahead of the next mismatch.
